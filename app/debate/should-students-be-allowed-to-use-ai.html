Debate · Should Students Use AI?

Should Students Be Allowed to Use AI in School?

A motion every school is arguing in real time. Below is a short sample round argued by an AI debater, a judge's verdict, and the strongest material on each side. Then take a side and run it yourself.

The clash in one line
Does AI build the skill or skip it? If it's a calculator for thinking, the question is which thinking we still want students to do by hand.

Sample round

The AI argues either side. Here it takes Pro first, then switches to Con.

Pro · allow it

Banning the tool students will use for the rest of their lives doesn't protect learning, it just makes school less honest about the world. The calculator didn't end math. It moved the lesson up the stack to when you apply the result.

Used in the open, AI becomes a tutor that is always available and never impatient, which matters most for the students who can't afford a private one. The equity gain is large and lands fastest on the kids with the least.

Con · restrict it

The calculator analogy breaks on one point: a calculator automates a step you've already mastered. AI automates the struggle itself, and the struggle is where learning happens. Outsource the first draft and you never build the muscle that writes the second.

The harm is quiet and compounding. A student who never sits in difficulty loses the one skill school exists to build, and you won't see the deficit until the test, the interview, or the job where the tool isn't allowed.

Pro · rebuttal

That assumes the assignment stays the same. It shouldn't. If a model can write the five-paragraph essay, the essay was testing the wrong thing. Move the task to defending the argument out loud, where the tool can't sit the exam for you.

Con's "struggle" point is real, so design for it. Allow AI for research and feedback, require the reasoning to be the student's own and assessed live. That keeps the muscle and the access.

Con · rebuttal

"Redesign every assessment" is the cost Pro keeps waving away. Most schools can't do that overnight, so in the real transition the default is quiet over-reliance, not Pro's well-supervised ideal.

Notice Pro's own fix proves the point: the safe version requires a human defending reasoning out loud. That's a restriction on unsupervised use, which is most of what Con asked for.

Judge · reason for decision

This round narrowed well. By the end both sides agreed AI should be allowed for research and feedback and restricted on the assessment that proves the reasoning is the student's own. The disagreement is feasibility versus principle.

Pro won the equity impact and the framing that bad assignments, not the tool, are the problem. Con won the strongest practical point, that the safe version depends on a redesign most schools can't ship quickly. On "allowed in principle," Pro takes it; on "allowed with no guardrails," Con does. The cleanest cases defend a specific policy, not a yes or no.

Strongest arguments on each side

Allow

  • An always-available tutor narrows the gap for students without private help.
  • Schools should teach the tools the real world runs on.
  • Bad assignments, not AI, are the problem; redesign the task.
  • Move assessment to live reasoning the tool can't fake.

Restrict

  • AI automates the struggle, and the struggle is where learning happens.
  • The skill deficit is invisible until a test the tool can't sit.
  • Safe use depends on a redesign most schools can't ship fast.
  • The default behavior is quiet over-reliance, not supervised use.
