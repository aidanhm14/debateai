The prep loop

Four steps. The whole thing fits in a lunch break.

1

Read the motion

Find the clash, not the topic. Write the one sentence the round actually turns on.

2

Build the case

Three arguments with warrants that hold. Each one walks magnitude, probability and timeframe.

3

Get attacked

A human opponent, or an AI that argues your format properly. This is the step people skip.

4

Read the ballot

Written RFD with speaker points and the one drill to run before your next round.

The hour before the round

Prep time collapses under pressure. This is the order that survives it, working backwards from the clock.

  1. 60 minutes out Read the motion twice, then close it.

    Say the clash out loud in one sentence. If you cannot, you have not understood the motion yet, and no amount of research fixes that.

  2. 45 minutes out Write three arguments. Not five.

    Five arguments means five shallow ones, and the shallow ones are where you lose. Three with real warrants beats five with tags.

  3. 30 minutes out Argue the other side out loud.

    The single highest-value prep move, and the one nobody does alone. Whatever you cannot answer here is what the opposition opens with.

  4. 15 minutes out Cut your worst argument.

    You already know which one it is. Carrying it costs you time in every speech and gives the other bench a free win on the flow.

  5. 5 minutes out Rehearse the first thirty seconds.

    Openings are the only part you can fully control. Land it clean and the rest of the speech settles.

Step three is the one that needs a second person. That is the gap this fills: pair with a real debater, or run a live voice round against the AI on a clock.

What prep usually misses

Prep alone rewards fluency, not accuracy. Rehearsing a case by yourself makes you smoother at saying it. It does nothing about whether the warrant holds, because nothing in the room is testing it.

The test is an opponent who takes the strongest version of the other side and goes after your weakest link. That is what a practice round is for, and it is the thing that is hardest to schedule at 11pm the night before a tournament.

Questions

How do I prepare for a debate in under an hour?

Work backwards from the clock. Read the motion and state the clash in one sentence, write three arguments rather than five, argue the opposing side out loud, cut your weakest argument, then rehearse your first thirty seconds. The full breakdown is in the hour-before section above.

Can I practice debate without a partner?

Yes. A voice round against the AI runs on a real clock, takes POIs, interrupts where the format allows it, and writes a judge ballot at the end. Casual formats finish in under ten minutes; APDA runs about forty-five, BP about an hour.

Is this free?

Free while in beta. Anonymous visitors get five AI rounds, and signing in with Google brings the total to ten. Human pairing works as a guest. Future pricing is published for reference and nothing is charged today.

Does it know my format properly?

Each format carries its own speech order, timings, citation rules, judging criteria and speaker scale. Policy gets tagged-card delivery, APDA stays impromptu with no fabricated citations, LD runs value and criterion, BP requires a real extension. Fifteen formats in total.

What does the judge ballot actually contain?

A written reason for decision that turns on the flow, speaker points on the scale your format uses, and one concrete drill to run before the next round. Not a summary that praises both sides.

Can my team or club use this for practice?

Yes. Coaches use it to fill the gap between scheduled practices, particularly for squads without enough people to run a full round. Details for programs are on the schools page.
