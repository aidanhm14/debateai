I.The thesis

What we are building.

A training environment and data layer for live adversarial reasoning, beginning with competitive debate.

Debatable is a voice-first system that lets a person take a side, argue it out loud against a format-trained AI, and receive a judge's ballot at the end. The AI plays a side. It interrupts. It drops POIs mid-clause. It writes a reason for decision the user can hand to a coach. Ten debate formats are argued natively: Policy, LD, PF, BP, APDA, WSDC, Asian Parli, Congress, MUN, Worlds.

Underneath the user-facing product is the part that compounds. Every signed-in round writes a labeled row to a Firestore generations collection: motion, side, format, system prompt, model output, user rating, judge RFD, full transcript. Rounds rated highly become exemplars for the next round on the same motion family. A nightly Haiku pass distills the top-rated rounds per format into a "patterns that work" block that prepends every subsequent generation. The AI on the same motion in three months is provably different from the AI on it today.

Three things compound at once and they do it independently. Frontier brains improve weekly and the product inherits the lift the day it ships. Voice latency drops toward human reaction time. The rated-rounds corpus grows every night. Most companies bet on one curve. The product sits at the intersection of three.

A general assistant is built to help you. A debater is built to take the side you didn't prep, and beat you with it.

II.The wedge

Why generic AI fails at adversarial reasoning.

Two objective functions. One optimizes to keep you in the conversation. The other optimizes to beat you in the round. They are not the same product with different prompts. They are different objective functions.

A general assistant earns its retention by being agreeable. That is the right call for an assistant; it is the wrong call for an opponent. A model trained on "did the user click thumbs-up" learns to hedge, smooth, and concede. A debater trained on "did the rebuttal land" learns to find the weakest joint in the case and stand there.

This is not a tone problem fixed by a system prompt. It is a training-signal problem. The corpus of live disagreement at adversarial intensity, labeled by format-aware judges, exists in roughly one place — the one where the rounds are actually being played.

Generic assistant
Built to help.
  • Agreeable by training. Hedges instead of pressing.
  • Shallow rebuttals. No strategic collapse pressure.
  • Treats every turn as standalone chat.
  • Optimized for retention, not for clash.
  • One register. "Harvard society" English on every motion.
Debatable
Built to beat you.
  • Adversarial cognition. Holds the line when you go quiet.
  • Pressure-tests the weakest joint of the case.
  • Argument-tree memory across six speeches.
  • Optimized for the ballot, not the conversation.
  • Ten format registers. Policy spreads, LD framework, BP whip, APDA impromptu, WSDC reply.

The labs will not pivot to this. A trillion-dollar incumbent cannot flip the reward signal of its flagship assistant from "be agreeable" to "disagree with the user." The retention drag is too steep and the brand bet is on warmth. The category the labs can build sits inside their existing product. The category we can build sits at the side of the road they cannot afford to take.

The wedge is debate because the format is the hardest to fake — circuit kids will detect a generic-LLM rebuttal in two speeches — and because a real ballot is unambiguous evidence the system works. If the product wins debaters first, the rest follows on the same engine. Lawyers running motion practice. Sales reps drilling objections. Founders rehearsing fundraising. Students preparing oral defense. Same loop, different room.

III.The flywheel

The corpus is the moat. The model isn't.

The loop that runs every night. Six steps. The AI on any given motion is measurably sharper this month than last because the rounds the user played are the rounds the next user trains against.
Six steps · closed loop · runs nightly at 04:00 UTC
  1. Round.Human vs. AI, or human vs. human with AI judging.
  2. Capture.Prompt, output, RFD, transcript, and user rating land in the generations collection.
  3. Rate.High-rated rounds and admin-weighted picks become exemplars.
  4. Distill.Nightly Haiku pass extracts "patterns that work" per format.
  5. Reinject.The block prepends tomorrow's system prompts.
  6. Sharper AI.Users face a stronger opponent and produce better rounds. The loop tightens.

Where the corpus stands today. 61 debaters have signed in with Google. The corpus is small; the compounding is real. The number that matters at this stage is rounds per signed-in cohort, not anonymous clicks, and that line is moving in the right direction. Most data moats begin underground. The question is whether the loop is running. The loop is running.

Why the labs cannot copy this. Their training signal is "did the user click thumbs-up." Ours is "did the rebuttal land." The first signal is on every page of the internet. The second exists only inside live disagreement at adversarial intensity, with format-aware judges in the room. A wrapper around a generic model does not compound. A corpus of rated live disagreement, format-tagged, accumulates value with every round.

IV.Voice + live cognition

Voice is the medium now because writing stopped being one.

When any sentence on the internet could have been written by a model in eight seconds, the written word loses most of its information content as a signal of who the writer is. The premium moves to the surfaces that still require a human in the room.

Three concrete shifts make this section non-speculative.

One — AI-written text is now ambient. Personal essays, cover letters, take-home assignments, technical writing samples, even legal briefs are increasingly first-drafted or polished by foundation models. The supply of competent prose has exploded; the marginal information content of any given paragraph has collapsed.

Two — institutions are noticing. Universities are rolling back the take-home essay. Top firms are reinstating live case interviews. Coding screens are moving from take-home repos back to live whiteboards. Investment partnerships now want founders on Zoom for thirty minutes before the memo. The thing every institution is reaching for is the same thing: a surface the candidate has to perform in real time, with no chance to ask a model.

Three — the surfaces that survive are oral. Job interviews. Sales calls. Board meetings. Negotiation. Fundraising. Press. Cross-examination. Viva defense. Each one is a live adversarial register where the listener can detect hesitation, hedge, drift. Each one is, in shape, a debate round.

This is why voice is not a UI layer on the product. Voice is the product. Server-side VAD already cuts the user off the way a real judge does. WebRTC keeps round-trip latency near the floor of human reaction time. The pause before a rebuttal, the POI dropped mid-clause, the interruption that lands on the weak warrant — those are not features. They are what makes a round a round. Within a year, a debater across the table will not be able to tell.

A foundation model can draft a memo in eight seconds. It cannot speak in your voice across a tense room. It cannot adjust persuasion in real time when the listener flinches. It cannot hold a position under POI pressure. As text generation commodifies, the residual premium moves to whatever still requires a human in the loop. We are building the gym for that premium.

Writing becomes cheap. Live communication becomes the credential. The same wave that makes essays free makes live cognition harder to fake and more valuable to certify.

V.The credential layer

Traditional tests measure prepared writing.
We measure live cognition.

A verifiable, transferable signal of communication competence in the AI era. Not a debate badge. A credential anyone whose job involves arguing in real time can carry with them.

Every round the product runs is, in shape, a structured assessment of how a person thinks out loud under adversarial pressure. The components are decomposable, measurable, and globally legible. The asymmetry that makes the credential valuable is simple: a cover letter could be the candidate or the model. A coding exercise could be the candidate or the model. A live oral defense at adversarial intensity cannot fake itself.

What we measure
  • Responsiveness. Time-to-answer under pressure.
  • Clarity under interruption. Signal retained when the floor moves.
  • Listening accuracy. Did the rebuttal address what was actually said.
  • Reasoning structure. Magnitude. Probability. Timeframe.
  • Persuasion calibration. Adjusts when the listener flinches.
  • Disagreement handling. Holds a position; concedes the right things.
  • Verbal confidence. Filler, hesitation, recovery from drift.
  • Format register. Right voice for the room.
  • Conversational adaptability. Reads the round, not the script.
The asymmetry, plainly

A résumé filters on credentials a foundation model could have written. A take-home essay filters on prose a foundation model could have polished. A coding exercise filters on solutions a foundation model could have completed.

A live, AI-graded round filters on something the foundation model cannot do for the candidate: think and argue under pressure, in real time, on a topic chosen for them.

The output is a signed transcript, a judge's RFD, and a numerical score across the nine components above. A URL anyone can verify. A credential a candidate can put at the top of a résumé that means something — globally, in any language, in any format.

VI.Beyond debate

The category goes global because the format is the wedge, not the limit.

Cross-language, cross-format adversarial reasoning is rare data in any language. The same loop that distills English Policy distills Hindi Asian Parli, Mandarin BP, Spanish MUN. Translation does not solve this. The training signal does.

Argument register in Lok Sabha parliamentary Hindi is not "English Asian Parli translated." It is its own genre, with its own rhetorical cadence, its own appeals, its own permissible interruptions. The Mandarin BP register in a Beijing-circuit final is not the Worlds register in English with a dictionary swapped in. Multilingual is not localization. It is the product.

The infrastructure to capture and distill those registers is the same infrastructure that already captures the English ones: every signed-in round writes to generations, ratings flow back through the loop, the nightly distill block lands in the next prompt. The corpus compounds in every language simultaneously, on the same engine.
