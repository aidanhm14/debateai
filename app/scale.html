For coaches · the practice of thinking

Clear thinking under question is a skill. It takes more reps than one coach can give.

The hardest thing you teach is composure when someone is picking at your argument in real time. It is also the most useful thing a student ever learns. DebateIt gives every debater on your roster the rounds, the pushback, and the written ballots that build it, on the nights you cannot be in the room.

That is rung one, and your team can use it this week. This page is where it leads: from practice rounds today up to open circuits and debate worth watching.

The reps gap

Every program runs into the same arithmetic.

It is not talent and it is not effort. It is time. One coach, a full roster, and a season that does not pause. Reps are the one thing you cannot manufacture, so they pool around the few students who already get the most.

Novices

need a low-stakes start

A first-year needs dozens of quiet rounds to stop freezing before a single point lands. You do not have dozens of patient partners to run them against.

Varsity

need a real test

Your strongest students have outgrown the room. They need an opponent who presses, takes points, and finds the weak link. Most weeks that opponent is you, once.

Everyone

needs feedback sooner

A note a week later teaches less than a ballot while the round is still warm. You cannot write forty by hand, so most rounds pass unjudged.

DebateIt does not stand in for you. It runs the rounds you cannot sit in, so your hours go to judgment and strategy, and to the student who actually needs a coach in the room.

Step three · a stage for the work

The round your principal can actually watch.

Programs get cut because nobody outside the room sees what happens inside it. A watchable round changes that. Alumni tune in. Parents see what their student does under pressure. The person deciding next year's budget sees a live event with your school's name on it.

Visibility is how a program survives and how it recruits. This is built to give careful argument the stage it has rarely had.

Step four · read the round

A flowing drill the whole room does at once.

Ask a room who won a round and half of them shrug. Give them a call to make and they watch closely: which warrant landed, which answer was a dodge, who carried the clash and not just the volume. Then the ballot posts, and they learn whether their read was right.

That is what The Floor is for. It runs on play credits, an AI settles the round, and it trains the exact judgment you want a flow to show. Engagement and adjudication practice, not a casino.

Where you can stop worrying

For students, this is play money. The Floor uses play credits, no card, nothing to win or lose. It is a way to keep score on how well a debater reads a round, the same instinct you grade on a flow.

Any real-stakes layer comes later, only where it is legal, and only for verified adults. Minors never touch redeemable cash, in this version or any future one. The aim is sharper judgment and a larger audience for your program, nothing more.

Straight answers

The questions you are already asking.

Will it teach my students bad habits?
It is built format by format. Policy gets tagged cards, LD gets value and criterion, BP gets extensions and whip, parli gets impromptu with no invented citations. It does not flatten every event into one generic style, which is the usual failure of a general chatbot.
Will it replace what I do?
No, and it is not trying to. It runs reps so students arrive at practice further along. Your time moves up to strategy, judgment, and the conversations only a coach can have.
Is the prediction layer gambling?
For students, no. The Floor is play credits, full stop. Any future redeemable layer is adults-only, legal-market only, and walled off from minors and competitors. What your team uses is a judging drill, not a bet.
Who built it?
A national APDA champion. Format accuracy is treated as a requirement, not a prompt trick, because the person who built it argued at the top of the circuit and knows where generic AI gets debate wrong.
