The mission · how big this gets

Debate has always been small by design.

A few people in a room. A handful of judges. A circuit you have to be invited into, fly to, and pay for. That is not the ceiling. It is the starting line.

The plan is one straight line: from small online tournaments you can enter from anywhere today, up to rounds the whole internet watches and backs a winner on. This page is that line.

Four rungs. Each one makes the next possible.

None of this is a leap. Every rung is the rung below it with more people watching. We are standing on the first one now.

Rung one · today

The first rung already works.

You can run a timed round right now against a real person matched from anywhere, or against an AI that interrupts and goes after your weakest line. An AI judge scores it and hands back a written ballot. That is a tournament you can enter from a laptop, with no flight and no fee.

Thousands of those rounds have already happened. Every one is a data point for the rungs above: proof the format works at a distance, proof a machine can judge consistently, proof people show up to argue when the door is actually open.

Rung three · mass viewing

Sport has an audience. Argument should too.

We watch people who are excellent under pressure: athletes, musicians, comedians working a room. A sharp debater taking a hostile question and turning it is the same kind of thing to watch. It has just never had a stage built for it.

The plan is to build that stage. The best rounds go live: a clear motion, two sides, an audience that can feel the round tilt in real time. Not a niche anyone has to translate. A spectacle anyone can follow.

Rung four · the markets

Watching is passive. Backing your read is not.

The moment you put something on a side, you watch differently. You stop spectating and start judging: which warrant actually landed, which rebuttal was a dodge, who is winning the clash and not just the volume. A market on the winner turns every viewer into a flow judge.

That layer exists in play-money form today. The Floor lets you back who wins across the round, the AI settles it, and a leaderboard ranks the readers. The honest long game is a real prize layer, built only where it is legal and clean.

How the money stays clean

Today it is free to play. The Floor runs on play credits, no card, no cash in or out. It is a way to keep score on how well you read a round.

Any real-stakes layer comes later, on one ledger with two tiers: play credits for everyone, and a redeemable prize tier only where it is legal. Minors never touch redeemable cash. The point is sharper reading and a louder audience, not a casino.

Why it compounds

Each rung feeds the one above it.

This is not four separate products. It is one loop that gets bigger the more it turns.

  1. More open rounds means more debaters with nowhere else to get reps like this.
  2. More debaters means more rounds worth watching, and a deeper bench of standout matchups.
  3. Rounds worth watching pull an audience that never debated, but will watch.
  4. An audience that can back a read turns viewers into judges with skin in it, and the market sharpens the watching.
  5. A sharper, louder audience makes a great round worth winning in public, which pulls in the next wave of debaters.

Pull the bottom rung and the whole thing rises. That is why the first job is simply to make open rounds great and get more of them happening. The stage and the markets are what that earns.
