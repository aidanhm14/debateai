Scaling debate practice is the problem of giving every student enough repetitions of arguing under questioning, when a program has one coach, a full roster, and a season that does not pause.[1] The limiting resource is not talent or effort but time: repetitions cannot be manufactured, so they pool around the few students who already get the most.

DebateIt addresses this in stages. The first — on-demand practice rounds with AI opponents and an AI judge — is available now; later stages add program-run circuits, watchable events, and an audience judging drill. This article describes the roadmap and its current status.

1The reps gap

Every program meets the same arithmetic: one coach, a full roster, and a season that does not pause. Because repetitions cannot be manufactured, three needs commonly go unmet:

  • Novices need a low-stakes start — dozens of quiet rounds before a single point lands, and few patient partners exist to run them.
  • Varsity need a real test — an opponent who presses, takes points, and finds the weak link; most weeks that opponent is the coach, once.
  • Everyone needs feedback sooner — a ballot while the round is warm teaches more than a note a week later, and forty ballots by hand is not feasible.

The stated aim is not to stand in for the coach but to run the rounds a coach cannot sit in, so coaching hours move to judgment, strategy, and the student who most needs a person in the room.

3What works today

Stage one is a practice room built around the formats students actually compete in, with their real rules on evidence, structure, and register.[2]

  • An opponent that questions back — AI debaters that press, take points of information, and look for the weak link; sixteen styles with distinct strengths and blind spots.
  • A ballot while it is warm — an AI judge that scores the round consistently and writes a reason for decision.
  • Real people when wanted — matchmaking against debaters anywhere, plus a board for scheduling rounds on a team’s own time.
  • Rounds spoken out loud — live voice rounds against the clock, with interruptions and points.

Formats supported: APDA, British Parliamentary, Policy, Lincoln–Douglas, Public Forum, World Schools, Asian Parliamentary, WSDC, Congress, Model UN, and Karl Popper.

4Watchable rounds

Programs are often cut because no one outside the room sees what happens inside it. A watchable round changes that: alumni tune in, parents see what a student does under pressure, and the person deciding next year’s budget sees a live event with the school’s name on it. In this roadmap, visibility is treated as how a program keeps its funding and recruits its next class, rather than as a marketing extra.

5Reading the round

Ask a room who won a round and half will shrug; give them a call to make and they watch closely — which warrant landed, which answer was a dodge, who carried the clash rather than the volume. When the ballot posts, they learn whether their read was right. This audience drill, called The Floor, runs on play credits and is settled by an AI judge; it trains the judgment a flow is meant to show.[3]

On play credits and minors

For students, this is play money. The Floor uses play credits: no card, nothing to win or lose. It is a way to keep score on how well a debater reads a round — the same instinct a coach grades on a flow.

Any real-stakes layer would come later, only where it is legal, and only for verified adults. Minors never touch redeemable cash, in this version or any future one.

6Common questions

Will it teach students bad habits?
It is built format by format: Policy gets tagged cards, Lincoln–Douglas gets value and criterion, British Parliamentary gets extensions and whip, parliamentary gets impromptu with no invented citations. It does not flatten every event into one generic style.
Will it replace the coach?
No. It runs reps so students arrive at practice further along. Coaching time moves up to strategy, judgment, and the conversations only a coach can have.
Is the prediction layer gambling?
For students, no. The Floor is play credits, full stop. Any future redeemable layer is adults-only, legal-market only, and walled off from minors and competitors.
Who built it?
A national APDA champion. Format accuracy is treated as a requirement rather than a prompt trick, because the builder argued at the top of the circuit and knows where generic AI gets debate wrong.

Stage one is usable by a team this week — timed rounds, pushback, and a ballot every time.
