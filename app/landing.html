Reason for decision

Motion: THBT we should ban for-profit prisons.

The round turns on one clash: whether the profit motive in private prison contracts produces measurably worse outcomes than the public-sector baseline, or whether both systems share the same pathologies and Pro is targeting the wrong cause.

Pro's case rests on three pillars. One, the Wisconsin-DOJ multi-state recidivism gap (9-15% higher in private facilities after controlling for risk profile). Two, the lobbying record on truth-in-sentencing legislation in Louisiana and Mississippi. Three, the minimum-occupancy clauses (90-95%) baked into the contracts themselves. Timotei concedes the first two in C1 and only partially contests the third.

Timotei's strongest beat is the common-pathology counter: public prisons run ~70% national recidivism, private ~75-80%, so the gap doesn't justify banning a sector that absorbs 8% of the federal population. The capacity-shock concern is real and well-warranted. Pro's response — "regulated public expansion over five years" — is reasonable but never costed.

The round is decided on impact magnitude. Pro establishes that the contract structure creates a class of advocate whose financial interest is in incarcerating more people for longer. That is not a tweakable variable; it is the business model. Timotei's reform-not-abolish frame would have won the round if she had run the counter-causal: public-sector growth absorbing the same lobbying capacity. The closing rebuttal never gets there.

Speaker points: Pro 28 for tight structure and closing the contract-clauses beat Timotei never re-engaged. Timotei 26 for the empirical work on capacity, losing ground on framing.

Philosophy at depth

Frameworks that hold up.

Most AI chatbots name-drop Rawls and stop there. Debate AI knows the difference between A Theory of Justice and Political Liberalism, which Kant formulation actually maps to the motion, and when to reach for Foucault's middle period vs. his late work on governmentality. Real warrants. And it tells you when philosophy is the wrong tool for the round.

A

Rawls, by period

Early Rawls (TJ): veil of ignorance, lexical priority, the two principles. Late Rawls (PL): overlapping consensus, public reason, reasonable pluralism. Picks the one that fits the resolution, not whichever you heard in lecture.

B

Kant, all three formulations

Universal law, humanity as end, kingdom of ends. The three rule in and out different conclusions on the same motion. The AI picks the one that actually grips the clash, not the one that sounds most impressive on paper.

C

50+ thinkers, paragraph-level

Foucault by period. Aristotle's Nicomachean vs. Politics. Marx's early manuscripts vs. Capital. Mill's harm principle in context. Nussbaum's capabilities. Sen on freedom. Pulled with passage fidelity, not a Wikipedia summary.

Sample, motion: THW make voting compulsory

AI Opp: Compulsory voting fails on public-reason grounds, not on the veil. Rawls in Political Liberalism §6 lets citizens hold incompatible comprehensive doctrines. Forcing them into a booth where the act of voting is the speech act compels participation in a scheme some reasonably reject. Public reason permits abstention as expressive content. The state can mandate showing up. It can't mandate that the showing-up count as endorsement, which is exactly what casting a vote does.

No throat-clearing. No "Rawls argued..." preface. The warrant is the paragraph.

Use it beyond rounds
i

Class essays

Paste the prompt. The AI builds a thesis, identifies the strongest framework for the question, generates the steelman objection, and gives you the response paragraph. Saves the four hours of trying to remember which Rawls cite goes where.

ii

Law school personal statements

The hardest move in a PS is grounding a personal claim in a philosophical commitment without sounding pretentious. The AI gives you the framework scaffold with attribution, then strips back to the personal register so the philosophy lifts the story instead of crushing it.

iii

Op-eds and Substack drafts

Opinion writing lives or dies on the warrant. The AI handles the philosophical move (why this principle, why now), you bring the news hook and the voice. Result: an op-ed that reads like the columnist already knows the literature, because they actually do.

iv

Interview prep

Rhodes, Marshall, Fulbright, Oxford PPE, Cambridge Tripos, law school admits panels. The interview committee will push your stated value to its breaking point. The AI plays the skeptic at exactly that level: cites the right counter-tradition, holds you to the specific commitment, then helps you respond without retreating.

v

Seminar cold-calls

Paste the assigned reading. The AI plays the Socratic prof who's done it more carefully than you have, and runs the move it'd run on you Tuesday morning. Works for philosophy seminars, law school cold-calls, MBA case prep. Five minutes the night before turns a panicked tomorrow into a calm walk-in.

vi

Thesis defense + conference Q&A

You can rehearse the talk. You can't rehearse the Q&A. The AI plays the person in the back who's read your literature, knows the subfield, and isn't kind. Quals committee, dissertation defense, post-talk panel question. The questions you survive here are the ones you don't choke on at the podium.

The AI

ChatGPT agrees with you. This doesn't. the AI is explicitly instructed to take the opposite side and pressure-test your reasoning. It finds the weak link, hits it, and forces you to defend or update your view.

The prompts also encode how actual competitive debaters argue: named historical anchors (not "studies show"), explicit concessions before attacks, multi-link warrant chains, weighing on time-frame and logical priority, and format-specific moves (two-voter PMR collapse in APDA, extension discipline in BP, etc.).

You're not getting a chatbot that happens to know about debate. You're getting a debater.

Four models, and you can pick: Claude (surgical and structured), GPT (creative and rhetorical), Gemini (fast and well-rounded), Grok (unfiltered and sharp). Each argues differently; the difference is noticeable after a few rounds.

Some features default to a specific model because it's strongest at that task (e.g. case generation defaults to Claude). You can override in any feature with a model toggle.

Why Debate

The highest-leverage skill almost nobody teaches.

Debate is the one room where being wrong is free and being convincing is everything. Someone throws an argument you've never heard, you have eight minutes to take it apart, and there's no Google and no teammate to lean on. Just you and whatever patterns you've built.

It rewires how you think. You stop asking "is this true?" and start asking "what would have to be true for this to be true, and would I bet on it?" You steelman before you strike, because the fastest way to lose a round is to attack the weakest version of your opponent's case. Most of the world conflates "I believe X" with "the strongest case for X." Debaters don't.

It pays, and the price is going up. The market is offering a premium for one skill: taking a wide-scope, technically dense idea and rendering it into something a room of strangers can hold in their heads. Not dumbing it down, compressing without losing the load-bearing parts. That's the job description of founders pitching VCs, PMs landing a feature, lawyers closing a case, policy aides briefing a senator with ninety seconds. The economy can't automate it.

Why this exists

The highest-leverage prep was never rehearsing the case. It was interrogating it. Looking for the cracks before someone else did.

Doing that alone is slow and biased. The blind spots stay blind. And asking a teammate to attack your case at 11 PM doesn't scale.

So this is the opponent I wanted as a debater. Paste a case and it returns the meanest honest counterargument a skeptical reader would make, ranks the attacks by severity, and flags the claims that won't survive scrutiny. Or run a full timed round in any of nine formats. Policy sounds like Policy. LD sounds like LD. APDA sounds like a circuit kid in a prep room with fifteen minutes left. Judge ballot at the end.

The wedge is simple. ChatGPT is sycophantic by training. Pushback is the product, not a feature.

That's what this is for.

Career destinations

The most overpowered prep skill few schools cash in on.

Competitive debate doesn't track to one career. It trains the substrate every analytical, client-facing job runs on: structured argument, evidence on a clock, handling pushback under pressure. Here's where high-school and college debaters disproportionately land, with the structural mapping for each.

01

Law

The canonical pipeline. A debate flow IS a legal brief. Cross-ex IS direct examination. Rebuttals are appellate work. Stephen Breyer was Stanford's debate ace before SCOTUS; Ted Cruz won the 1992 NSDA Extemporaneous Speaking nationals before Harvard Law. Most large-firm litigation associates trace their structured-argument skill back to high-school or college debate.

02

Management consulting

A McKinsey, BCG, or Bain case interview is a five-minute parli round. Identify the resolution, set the framework, give three impacts, take pushback, sharpen the conclusion. Top firms over-recruit from APDA, BP, and Worlds programs at Ivies and Oxbridge for exactly this reason: the case-interview pattern matches the round flow one-to-one.

03

Politics & government

The most visible pipeline. Bill Clinton (Georgetown), Barack Obama (Punahou and Occidental), Pete Buttigieg, and most modern US presidents debated. The Oxford Union president list reads like a UK Cabinet roster: Boris Johnson, William Hague, Michael Heseltine. State AGs, congressional staff, and campaign managers over-index for ex-debaters.

04

Investment banking & PE

Less direct than consulting; the value is in the pitch. The analyst-to-MD narrative is a structured argument with a clock. Goldman, Morgan Stanley, KKR, Blackstone associates increasingly come from debate programs because the LP call and the IC presentation are both adversarial argument under deadline.

05

Tech founders

Under-recognized but consistent. A seed pitch IS a five-minute case. Investor Q&A IS cross-ex. Board meetings are whip speeches. YC partners over-index for ex-debater founders because conviction-under-pressure is the actual founder skill, not the slide design.

06

Journalism, policy, academia

Op-ed columnists, think-tank fellows, and academic philosophers disproportionately have debate backgrounds. Brookings, RAND, AEI, Cato, CFR hire heavily from this lane. The reason is identical to law: structured argument with sourced evidence + handling pushback in real time IS the job.

The structural claim: the jobs above pay well because they reward the same five skills, in different uniforms. Structured argument. Evidence on a clock. Cross-examination. Adapting when the room pushes back. Closing under time pressure. Debate practices all five, every round. The AI that argues back lets you practice them in 15-minute blocks instead of two-hour tournaments.
